I'm an IBM-certified Design Thinking Co-Creator. I'd been searching for UX courses, and stumbled upon IBM's series instead. Far as I'm concerned, 'twas serendipity!

As a technical writer, it's my job to focus on user goals in all my tasks. As a remote worker though, I do run the risk of missing outliers while planning doc structures or scope. Then there's the actual writing, reviews, improvs...you know the drill.
So if stakeholders' meetings threaten drastic edits to those perfectly-crafted sentences and info architecture layouts that took weeks, the rebel in you sees red. It's natural.

Luckily, that's exactly what design thinking can help fix, by making you focus on empathy, human-centered solutions, and continuous improvement.
If I had to pick just one aspect of design thinking that (sole) writers must imbibe, it's this: Restless Reinvention.

Treat everything like a prototype so you can quickly improve solutions.

Design thinking suggests that every team member - across job roles - should collaborate at every stage of product development (and technical writing, by extension). Startups are great for that; giving dev teams a say in doc design/planning/publishing makes them actually care about docs. (True story!)

Mostly though, by not aiming for a 'perfect' draft, I spend less time wondering about possible solutions, and more time actually testing ones that work.

The only constant in life is change. ~Heraclitus
