I'm an [IBM-certified Design Thinking Co-Creator](https://www.youracclaim.com/badges/231db2da-f36e-44e0-8cdc-af5ab45c6db0/public_url). Far as I'm concerned, 'twas serendipity! I'd been googling for UX courses, and stumbled upon IBM's [series](https://www.ibm.com/design/thinking/page/courses/Practitioner) instead. 

As a technical writer, it's my job to focus on user goals in all my tasks. As a remote worker though, I do run the risk of missing outliers while planning doc structures or scope. Then there's the actual writing, reviews, improvs...you know the drill.  

So if stakeholders' meetings threaten drastic edits to those perfectly-crafted sentences and info architecture layouts that took weeks, the rebel in you sees red. It's natural. 

Luckily, that's exactly what design thinking can help fix. It suggests that every team member - across job roles - should collaborate at every stage of product development (and technical writing, by extension). Startups are great for that; giving dev teams a say in doc design/planning/publishing makes them care about the docs. (True story!)  

Design thinking reminds us to focus on empathy, human-centered solutions, and continuous improvement.
 
If I have to pick one aspect of design thinking that (sole) writers must imbibe, it's this: *Fail fast and cheap*. By *not* aiming for a 'perfect' draft, I spend less time theorizing about possible solutions, and more time actually improving ones that work.  

**The only constant in life is change. ~Heraclitus**
